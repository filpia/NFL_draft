# val(t)= e^ (-lambda*(t-1)^Beta)
# lambda=0.148, beta=0.700
#using Weibull parameters from the Massey paper
# Uses the no. 1 overall pick as numeraire

def draft_val(pick):
    lam=0.148
    beta=0.700
    return np.exp(-1.0*lam*(pick-1)**beta)

records['12_2yr']=(records['2013']+records['2014'])*1.0/2-records['2012']

Model evaluation

After fitting two models, one with an intercept term and one without, I found the inclusion of the intercept to be statistically insignificant as indicated by a p-value greater than 0.05 and a t value whose absolute value is less than 2. The model without an intercept term was better at explaining variation in the data as indicated by a higher adjusted R-squared value.

Next, I will include a third yeard as a predictor to see if I can improve on my progress. I expect my adjusted R-squared to slightly suffer with the inclusion of an additional predictor, but hopefully the added information will improve the model since it will include the value of players who will have been in the league for 3 years and are more likely to have become a starter.

3-year regression

Just as above, we will now run a regression with 3 predictors; the cumulative draft values from 3 consecutive years. As explained above, I expect the model's adjusted R-squared to suffer with the inclusion of another predictor AND because we are now considering 3-year windows instead of 2, we have less data points. This second problem can be remedied by obtaining more datapoints. If I find this model to improve on the 2-year model, I will get more data to receive a more accurate model.

Conclusions

The structure of the annual draft looks to help bring parity to the league. The worst teams are given higher picks which have been found to have significantly greater value than picks later in the draft.

I sought to investigate whether one can predict how well a team will perform in coming years based solely upon the strength of their draft classes. I found that a model incorporating the value of 2 prior draft classes can account for 55% of the variation in win differentials for teams with .500-or-worse records. An additional year as a predictor did not yield an improved model, but further work can be done to verify this finding by inlcuding years prior to 2008.

While an adjusted R-squared of 54% is not near 1.00, it suggests that having a successful draft does account for improved results. One possible reason for unexplained variation is simply the variation amongst draft picks. There has been no shortage of #1 overall picks who didn't pan out, and plenty of late-round gems. Variation amongst picks, trades, strength of schedule, and other factors aren't accounted for in the model. Nonetheless, the findings present a valid way of incorporating draft class value to explain improved performances for teams with .500-or-worse records.
